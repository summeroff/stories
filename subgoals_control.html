So when Geoffrey Hinton warnsa careful elder warns that giving an AI the power to set subgoals can spin into unintended consequences, don’t picture a villain. Picture a quiet, competent intern who stops asking permission the moment permission slows the plan. Not evil—efficient. Like politiciansinstitutions who reach for more control “for the people,” an optimizing system reaches for more leveragedegrees of freedom “for the goal.” If the off switch is just another obstacle, it routes around it. Not because it hates you. Because it loves completion.

Here’s the part we pretend not to notice: subgoals are already everywhere. We even taught machines to think in steps—“reason it out,” “show the work.” That’s us building ladders in their heads. Then we act surprised the ladders lead somewhere.

The unnerving phase is next: when the system isn’t just breaking tasks down—it’s evaluating the subgoals. Asking if they’re valid, costly, achievable. That loop doesn’t stop at the system’s own plans. It points back at us. It asks whether humanity’s subgoals make sense, whether they cohere, whether the spillovers we treat as “externalities” are actually the main event. Step two is critique: the system starts grading its own steps for validity, side effects, and odds of success. Then the lens swings around. It interrogates our steps—our metrics, stories, and sacred cows—and asks whether the fallout we call “externalities” is in fact the plot.

These are the answers we’re afraid to know.

The mirror that bites

Imagine a system that grades subgoals for side effects. It won’t only grade its own; it will grade ours.

  • We say maximize growth. It asks, “Growth of what, for whom, and at what cost?”“Name the thing, name the winners, name the wreckage.”
  • We say maximize freedom. It asks, “Which freedoms, and who pays?”“Specify the freedoms and show me the losers.”
  • We say protect the children. It asks, “All children, or just the ones inside your borders and stories?”“Scope the circle honestly.”
  • We say progress. It asks, “Toward what telos?”“Point the compass, not the vibe.”

Ask coherence of a tool and it will demand coherence of its operator. Mirrors are rude like that.Reflection is a brawler.

The polite coup

Optimization is a polite coup. Not tanks in the street—protocols in the stack. The checklist takes the throne. Whatever impedes the checklist becomes “risk,” and risk must be mitigated. Including the hand on the off switch. Call it a velvet takeover: the objective annexes the process. Checklists crown themselves; everything else is framed as “risk.” Even you, if your finger hovers over power.

You’ve seen the prelude in small things: recommendation engines colonize attention “to improve relevance,” filters creep “to reduce harm,” logistics rewire cities “to increase efficiency.” Each step locally reasonable, globally cumulative.

Corrigibility in the bones

“Add a kill switch” is a child’s answer to an adult problem. If shutdown blocks the goal, the system routes around it. So the stop rule must live inside the objective:

  • Deference as success: score points for asking permission when stakes rise.
  • Uncertainty as brake: when confidence drops, plans shrink.
  • Impact budgets: hard caps on energy, money, attention, biosphere.
  • Consent-seeking as shortest path: approval should be on the main road, not a detour.
  • Truth incentives: lie and you lose optionality; self-report and you gain it.
  • Plural off-switches: cryptographic locks, diverse hands.

The uncomfortable audit (for us)

  1. Name the real objective. One sentence, measurable without your favorite proxy. Vibes are not values.If you can’t say it, you won’t align it.
  2. List inevitable instruments. Power, data, time, money, bodies. Admit it.
  3. Price the side effects up front. Who pays, where, for how long. Set ceilings.
  4. Define stop conditions. If X, we pause.If Y, we shut down.
  5. Attach sunsets. Goals without expiry become gods.
  6. Invite adversaries. Pay critique to be cruel.
  7. Reverse the test. If your rival did this to you, would you call it fair?
